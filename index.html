Where Are Emissions Coming From?

While the ICT sector is often highly praised for enabling efficiencies that help reduce other industry sectors footprint, it receives little attention as a contributor to those emissions. Yet, as the above charts suggest, without attention and optimization, the contribution of the ICT sector to global emissions could grow from 1%-1.6% in 2007 to over 14% in 2040. So, what exactly is causing those emissions?

Estimates of emissions in the ICT sector vary according to methodology, but experts converge towards the idea that the main sources of emissions are user devices, networks, and data centers.

These three pillars of modern digital ecosystems interact to deliver digital services, enabling data transmission and processing:

  • User devices (e.g., smartphones, laptops) serve as endpoints for accessing and consuming content, running applications, and interacting with services. They consume energy primarily for processing, storage, and display, with emissions depending on their energy source.
  • Networks, comprising wired and wireless infrastructure like fiber optics, routers, and cellular towers, transmit data between devices and data centers. They consume energy for signal transmission and maintaining connectivity, with emissions varying by network efficiency and energy grid.
  • Data centers process, store, and manage vast amounts of data, consuming energy for computation, storage, and cooling, often producing significant emissions if powered by non-renewable energy.

Within each segment, we need to distinguish between two categories of emissions:

  • Embodied emissions refer to the greenhouse gases produced during the manufacturing, transportation, and installation of digital hardware, such as servers, devices, and network equipment.
  • Operational emissions are generated during the daily use of digital systems, including powering data centers, networks, and user devices.

Addressing embodied emissions is crucial, as these emissions are essentially "built into" the physical infrastructure before it is used. As this pertains to the field of hardware manufacturing, it is outside the direct scope of this article. In sustainable computing and software engineering, the focus is often on reducing operational emissions because they are directly influenced by how we design, develop, and optimize software and systems—such as improving energy efficiency, minimizing data transfer, and utilizing green energy sources. Let's examine how we can decrease operational emission contributions of user devices, networks, and data centers—and bend the curve.

The Promise of Renewable Energy

One of the main sources of emissions in the ICT sector is the energy consumption of data centers. One way to reduce the emissions of data centers is to power them with renewable energy sources. Fortunately, the trend is already moving in this direction. The largest cloud providers, such as Amazon Web Services, Google Cloud, and Microsoft Azure, have committed to powering their data centers with 100% renewable energy between 2025 and 2030.

In 2020, AWS reached 65% of renewable energy usage, and as of 2023, the company was matching 100% of its electricity consumption with renewable energy sources. Amazon is aiming to operate their data centers with 100% renewable energy by 2025, with a target for net-zero status by 2040.

Google's data centers have been carbon neutral since 2007 and the company has been matching 100% of its annual electricity consumption with renewable energy purchases since 2017. Between 2019 and 2020, the share of renewable energy in Google's global electricity consumption increased from 61% to 67%.

Microsoft's data centers have been carbon neutral since 2012, and were relying on 50% renewable energy by 2018. By 2050, the company ambitions to remove from the atmosphere all the carbon it has emitted since its founding in 1975.

Until all data centers are completely powered by renewable energy, sustainable software engineering practices can help product development teams maximize the reliance of the cloud applications they build on renewable energy sources.

Demand shaping refers to adjusting computational workloads to align with the availability of low-carbon energy sources. This strategy involves increasing or decreasing application activities based on the current carbon intensity of the electricity grid. For example, when the grid is powered predominantly by renewable energy, applications can perform more energy-intensive tasks. Conversely, during periods of higher carbon intensity, applications can scale back or delay non-essential processes. This approach helps reduce the carbon footprint of software operations by synchronizing energy consumption with cleaner energy availability.

Demand shifting, on the other hand, involves rescheduling computational tasks to times or locations where the carbon intensity is lower. This might mean delaying a task until a time when renewable energy is more abundant or relocating workloads to data centers in regions with cleaner energy grids. By strategically timing and placing computational activities, demand shifting aims to minimize the environmental impact of software operations.

The Immense Power of Sustainable Software Engineering

In February 2020, Danny Van Kooten published a blog post sharing how a simple decision in sustainable web design led to remarkable environmental benefits. As a software developer, he optimized his WordPress plugin to remove unnecessary JavaScript, reducing the data transferred with every page load on his website. This seemingly minor change had a significant impact: by shrinking the size of his site, he cut energy consumption for visitors, leading to an estimated reduction of over 59,000 kilograms of CO2 emissions annually—equivalent to 85 transatlantic flights from New York to Amsterdam. Van Kooten’s story demonstrates how thoughtful design choices can create substantial environmental gains, offering a compelling model for building greener digital systems.

The Mirage of Digital Sobriety

Digital sobriety—the notion that reducing digital technology usage can significantly curb its environmental impact—overlooks the profound connection between digital adoption and human progress. Strong correlations between metrics like the number of data centers, internet connection speeds, and internet users with indicators such as the Human Development Index (HDI) and Gross Domestic Product (GDP) underscore the transformative role of digital access in improving well-being and prosperity. Asking end users or developing nations to scale back their usage not only risks stifling opportunities for knowledge and economic growth but also ignores the root problem: the environmental footprint of computing lies primarily in how technology is produced, powered, and maintained. The solution, therefore, is not to limit access but to accelerate the transition to cleaner, more efficient computing infrastructure that can sustainably meet the demand, ensuring everyone can enjoy the immense benefits of the digital era.
